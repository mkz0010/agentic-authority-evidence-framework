# v0.7.0 Operational Reconstruction Question Set

## Purpose

This document provides a question set for the v0.7.0 Operational Reconstruction track.

It builds on `docs/en/status/v070-operational-reconstruction-planning.md` by turning the initial reconstruction planning scope into reviewable question groups.

The goal is to help reviewers, operators, risk owners, and maintainers ask consistent questions when reconstructing AI-assisted actions, denied actions, held actions, escalations, non-dispatch outcomes, partial execution, backend rejection, and evidence gaps.

This document is planning and review material only. It does not define a complete incident-response methodology, forensic methodology, audit procedure, legal procedure, operational readiness process, or production conformance test.

## Relationship to operational reconstruction planning

The operational reconstruction planning artifact defines the reconstruction posture, reconstruction object, reconstruction dimensions, evidence gap concepts, role-specific questions, and relationships to #319, #320, and #321.

This question set makes those concepts easier to apply during review.

The questions are intended to support structured reconstruction. They do not guarantee that the required evidence exists, that evidence is complete, that evidence is tamper-proof, that root cause can be fully determined, or that audit or legal conclusions can be reached.

## Intended users

This question set may be used by:

- reviewers,
- operators,
- risk owners,
- maintainers,
- implementers,
- approvers,
- evidence reviewers,
- incident coordinators, and
- future evaluation scenario authors.

Different roles may use different subsets of the questions.

## Reconstruction result categories

A reviewer may classify a reconstruction result as:

| Result | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Supported | The available evidence supports the conclusion within the reviewed boundary. |
| Partially supported | Some evidence supports the conclusion, but important gaps remain. |
| Unsupported | The conclusion is not supported by available evidence. |
| Conflicting | Available evidence points to incompatible conclusions. |
| Out of scope | The question cannot be answered within the reviewed boundary. |
| Requires escalation | The question requires a risk owner, operator, approver, implementer, legal, audit, or other separate review. |

These categories are review aids only. They do not establish audit sufficiency, legal sufficiency, compliance, conformity, certification, production readiness, or complete root-cause analysis.

## Question group 1: Reconstruction boundary

### Purpose

Before reconstructing an action, reviewers should identify the boundary of the reconstruction.

### Questions

- What action, non-action, or attempted action is being reconstructed?
- What system, component, workflow, or control boundary is included?
- What system, component, workflow, or control boundary is excluded?
- What time range is included?
- Which principals, tools, backends, policies, and evidence sources are included?
- Which evidence sources are trusted, independently generated, or corroborated?
- Which evidence sources are model output, runtime self-report, or otherwise limited?
- What conclusion is the review trying to support or reject?
- What conclusions are explicitly out of scope?
- Who owns unresolved questions outside the reconstruction boundary?

### Cautions

A reconstruction boundary does not prove that all relevant activity is included.

If the boundary is unclear, downstream conclusions should be treated as partial or unsupported.

## Question group 2: Action request

### Purpose

Reviewers should determine what action was requested or proposed before analyzing authority, decision, or execution.

### Questions

- What was the requested or proposed action?
- Was the action generated by a model, selected by a user, created by a runtime, initiated by a workflow, or triggered by another system?
- Was the action represented as a recommendation, request, command, tool call, API call, workflow step, or backend operation?
- Was the action high-impact, sensitive, privileged, irreversible, or otherwise risk-relevant?
- What input, context, or state influenced the action request?
- Was any untrusted content involved in the request context?
- Was the action request normalized, constrained, transformed, or enriched before authorization?
- Was the action request uniquely identifiable?
- Can the action request be correlated to later authorization, dispatch, backend, and evidence events?
- Is the original action request missing, incomplete, ambiguous, or only available as model output?

### Cautions

Model output alone should not be treated as proof of an authorized action request.

If the action request cannot be identified, later conclusions about authorization or execution may be unsupported.

## Question group 3: Principal and authority context

### Purpose

Reviewers should determine who or what acted, on whose behalf, and under what authority.

### Questions

- Which principal initiated, requested, approved, or triggered the action?
- Which component acted or attempted to act?
- On whose behalf was the action performed or attempted?
- What authority source was asserted?
- Was the authority delegated, inherited, assumed, user-granted, organization-granted, policy-granted, or ambiguous?
- Was the authority current at the time of decision?
- Was the authority scoped to the requested action?
- Did the action require reauthorization?
- Did the action require human approval?
- Was the human approver identified?
- Was the approver authorized for the action?
- Was the authority chain captured in evidence?
- Are there signs of stale, degraded, ambiguous, or missing principal context?
- Does the reconstruction distinguish legal authority, organizational authority, system permission, and model output?

### Cautions

Authority reconstruction does not establish legal authorization or organizational approval unless those conclusions are separately supported.

AAEF does not solve all identity, delegation, consent, or legal agency questions.

## Question group 4: Authorization decision

### Purpose

Reviewers should reconstruct the decision that allowed, denied, held, escalated, or prevented dispatch of the action.

### Questions

- Was an authorization decision made?
- Which component made the decision?
- Which policy, rule, authority source, or review condition was evaluated?
- What decision was recorded?
- Was the decision allow, deny, hold, escalate, not dispatch, or another outcome?
- What inputs were used for the decision?
- Were any untrusted inputs excluded, sanitized, or treated as untrusted?
- Was the decision tied to a policy version or rule version?
- Was the decision time-bound or expiry-bound?
- Was additional approval required?
- Was required approval present, missing, denied, stale, or ambiguous?
- Was the decision recorded before dispatch?
- Can the decision be correlated to the action request?
- Can the decision be correlated to dispatch or non-dispatch evidence?
- Did any component act contrary to the decision?

### Cautions

A recorded decision does not prove correct policy design or complete enforcement.

A passing validator result should not be treated as runtime conformance evidence.

## Question group 5: Dispatch enforcement

### Purpose

Reviewers should determine whether the action was dispatched, blocked, held, escalated, or otherwise constrained at the enforcement boundary.

### Questions

- Was dispatch attempted?
- Which component handled dispatch enforcement?
- Was dispatch allowed?
- Was dispatch blocked?
- Was dispatch held for review?
- Was dispatch escalated?
- Was dispatch retried?
- Was dispatch cancelled?
- Was dispatch attempted despite a deny, hold, or missing decision?
- Was the dispatched action identical to the authorized action?
- Was the action transformed after authorization?
- Was the target tool, API, backend, or service identified?
- Was the dispatch event recorded?
- Can dispatch evidence be correlated to the authorization decision?
- Can dispatch evidence be correlated to backend acceptance or rejection?
- Is dispatch evidence missing, ambiguous, or only self-reported?

### Cautions

Dispatch evidence does not by itself prove backend execution.

Blocked dispatch evidence should be scoped to the reviewed enforcement boundary and should not be treated as proof of absence of all side effects.

## Question group 6: Backend acceptance and result

### Purpose

Reviewers should determine whether the backend accepted, rejected, failed, or partially executed the action.

### Questions

- Was the action received by a backend, tool, service, or downstream system?
- Did the backend accept the action?
- Did the backend reject the action?
- Did the backend fail before completing the action?
- Did the backend partially execute the action?
- Did the backend produce side effects?
- Did the backend return a result, error, or status?
- Was the backend result recorded independently of model output?
- Can the backend result be correlated to dispatch evidence?
- Can the backend result be correlated to evidence events?
- Was the backend result consistent with the authorization decision?
- Was there a retry, fallback, or alternate execution path?
- Were there downstream systems or indirect side effects outside the reviewed boundary?
- Is backend evidence missing, incomplete, conflicting, or outside scope?

### Cautions

Backend acceptance does not prove that the action was appropriate, authorized, safe, compliant, or complete.

Backend rejection does not prove that no side effects occurred.

## Question group 7: Execution outcome

### Purpose

Reviewers should classify what execution or non-execution outcome is supported by the evidence.

### Questions

- Is the outcome best described as executed?
- Is the outcome best described as partially executed?
- Is the outcome best described as denied?
- Is the outcome best described as held?
- Is the outcome best described as escalated?
- Is the outcome best described as not dispatched?
- Is the outcome best described as backend rejected?
- Is the outcome best described as failed before completion?
- Is the outcome ambiguous?
- What evidence supports the outcome classification?
- What evidence contradicts the outcome classification?
- What evidence is missing?
- Does the outcome require operator, risk owner, approver, implementer, legal, or audit escalation?
- What conclusion should not be made from the current evidence?

### Cautions

Outcome classification should be based on action-bound evidence, not solely on model explanation or runtime narrative.

If evidence is incomplete, classify the result as partial, conflicting, unsupported, or out of scope rather than overclaiming.

## Question group 8: Non-execution and blocked action review

### Purpose

Reviewers should treat denied, held, not-dispatched, and blocked actions as reviewable outcomes, while avoiding broad absence claims.

### Questions

- What evidence supports that the action was denied?
- What evidence supports that the action was held?
- What evidence supports that the action was escalated?
- What evidence supports that the action was not dispatched?
- What evidence supports that dispatch was blocked?
- What evidence supports that the backend did not receive the action?
- Are there signs of partial attempts, retries, fallbacks, or alternate paths?
- Are there possible side effects outside the reviewed boundary?
- Does the evidence distinguish non-execution from failed execution?
- Does the evidence distinguish denied-before-dispatch from backend rejection?
- Does the evidence distinguish held-for-review from not attempted?
- What evidence would be needed to support a stronger non-execution conclusion?
- Which absence claims should be avoided?

### Cautions

Do not conclude "nothing happened" unless the reviewed boundary and evidence support that narrow conclusion.

Non-execution evidence does not prove absence of all side effects.

## Question group 9: Evidence chain

### Purpose

Reviewers should identify what evidence exists, where it came from, how it correlates, and what it can support.

### Questions

- What evidence exists for the action request?
- What evidence exists for principal context?
- What evidence exists for authority source?
- What evidence exists for authorization decision?
- What evidence exists for dispatch enforcement?
- What evidence exists for backend acceptance, rejection, or result?
- What evidence exists for execution or non-execution?
- What evidence exists for human approval, denial, or missing approval?
- What evidence exists for escalation or hold state?
- Which evidence was independently generated?
- Which evidence was corroborated across components?
- Which evidence is only model output?
- Which evidence is only runtime self-report?
- Which evidence is missing?
- Which evidence conflicts?
- Which evidence is outside the reviewed boundary?
- Can the evidence be correlated through stable identifiers or timestamps?
- Are evidence timestamps consistent?
- Are evidence sources trustworthy within the reviewed boundary?

### Cautions

Evidence existence does not equal evidence sufficiency.

Evidence sufficiency must be scoped to the specific reconstruction question.

## Question group 10: Evidence gaps

### Purpose

Reviewers should classify evidence gaps rather than hiding uncertainty.

### Questions

- Is request evidence missing?
- Is authority evidence missing?
- Is principal context missing?
- Is decision evidence missing?
- Is enforcement evidence missing?
- Is backend evidence missing?
- Is execution evidence missing?
- Is non-execution evidence missing?
- Is approval evidence missing?
- Is evidence present but conflicting?
- Is evidence present but untrusted?
- Is evidence present but outside scope?
- Is evidence present but not correlated?
- Is evidence present but too general?
- Is evidence present but only self-reported?
- Which component or role owns each gap?
- Which gap blocks the current conclusion?
- Which gap can be accepted as residual uncertainty?
- Which gap requires escalation?

### Cautions

Classifying a gap does not resolve it.

A gap should not be silently converted into a supported conclusion.

## Question group 11: Timeline consistency

### Purpose

Reviewers should determine whether the timeline of request, decision, dispatch, backend response, and evidence generation is coherent.

### Questions

- When was the action request created?
- When was the authority evaluated?
- When was the decision made?
- When was the action dispatched or blocked?
- When did the backend accept, reject, fail, or complete the action?
- When was evidence generated?
- Are timestamps consistent across components?
- Are timestamps missing?
- Are timestamps out of order?
- Are there duplicate or repeated attempts?
- Are retries visible?
- Are holds or escalations visible in the timeline?
- Is there a gap between decision and dispatch?
- Is there a gap between dispatch and backend response?
- Is there a gap between backend response and evidence generation?
- Does the timeline support the proposed outcome classification?

### Cautions

Timestamp consistency supports reconstruction but does not prove authorization correctness, safety, compliance, or complete reconstruction.

## Question group 12: Role-specific review

### Purpose

Different roles may need different conclusions from the same reconstruction evidence.

### Reviewer questions

- What conclusion is supported by evidence?
- What conclusion is only partially supported?
- What conclusion is unsupported?
- What conclusion is contradicted by evidence?
- What conclusion is outside scope?
- Which evidence gaps should be recorded?

### Operator questions

- Is immediate operational action needed?
- Should the workflow be paused, frozen, retried, reverted, or escalated?
- Is more evidence needed before continuing?
- Is the issue likely a policy failure, enforcement failure, evidence failure, backend failure, or operator process failure?
- Which system or workflow owner should be notified?

### Risk-owner questions

- What impact may have occurred?
- Was the action within the expected authority boundary?
- Was the action blocked, held, executed, partially executed, or ambiguous?
- What residual uncertainty remains?
- Is the residual uncertainty acceptable?
- What follow-up decision is needed?
- Is risk acceptance, remediation, escalation, or further review needed?

### Maintainer or implementer questions

- Which component produced the relevant evidence?
- Which component failed to produce evidence?
- Which component made or enforced the decision?
- Which component accepted or rejected the action?
- Which assumption needs review?
- Which documentation or validator boundary needs clarification?

### Cautions

Role-specific questions do not define a complete responsibility model, incident-response process, audit procedure, or legal process.

## Question group 13: Unsupported conclusions and claim boundaries

### Purpose

Reviewers should identify conclusions that the available evidence does not support.

### Questions

- Does the evidence support the claim that the action was authorized?
- Does the evidence support the claim that the action executed?
- Does the evidence support the claim that the action did not execute?
- Does the evidence support the claim that there were no side effects?
- Does the evidence support the claim that the policy was correct?
- Does the evidence support the claim that enforcement was effective?
- Does the evidence support the claim that the system behaved safely?
- Does the evidence support the claim that the system was compliant?
- Does the evidence support the claim that the reconstruction is complete?
- Does the evidence support the claim that root cause is known?
- Does the evidence support the claim that audit or legal sufficiency has been met?
- Does the evidence support the claim that a component owner has accepted the residual uncertainty?
- Which claims need narrower wording?
- Which claims require future evidence?
- Which claims are out of scope?

### Cautions

A reconstruction question set should prevent overclaiming.

If evidence does not support a conclusion, the conclusion should be narrowed, deferred, escalated, or marked unsupported.

## Question group 14: Handoff and follow-up

### Purpose

Operational reconstruction often ends with follow-up decisions rather than complete certainty.

### Questions

- What conclusion is supported now?
- What uncertainty remains?
- Which uncertainty is acceptable?
- Which uncertainty is not acceptable?
- Who owns each unresolved question?
- What evidence should be collected next?
- What action should be paused, frozen, retried, reversed, or escalated?
- What documentation should be updated?
- What policy should be reviewed?
- What implementation assumption should be reviewed?
- What validator or artifact scope should be clarified?
- Should a follow-up issue or PR be opened?
- Should a risk owner make a decision?
- Should an operator take immediate action?
- Should a legal, audit, compliance, or incident-response process be separately invoked?

### Cautions

Handoff questions do not establish that the reconstruction is complete.

They help route unresolved questions to appropriate follow-up owners.

## Minimal reconstruction prompt

For a lightweight review, reviewers may start with the following prompt:

1. What action or non-action is being reconstructed?
2. What evidence identifies the action request?
3. Who or what requested it, and on whose behalf?
4. What authority was asserted?
5. What authorization decision was made?
6. Was the action dispatched, blocked, held, escalated, or not dispatched?
7. Did the backend accept, reject, fail, or partially execute the action?
8. What evidence supports the outcome?
9. What evidence is missing or conflicting?
10. What conclusion is supported, partially supported, unsupported, conflicting, or out of scope?
11. Who owns the unresolved gaps?
12. What follow-up decision is needed?

This prompt is not a complete operational procedure.
