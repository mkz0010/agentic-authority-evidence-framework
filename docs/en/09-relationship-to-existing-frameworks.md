# 09. Relationship to Existing Frameworks

AAEF is intended to complement existing AI governance, security, identity, and agentic AI frameworks.

It should not be positioned as a replacement for established or emerging work.

## Positioning

AAEF focuses on:

- delegated authority,
- policy-enforced action boundaries,
- verifiable evidence,
- revocable trust,
- and assessment of high-impact agentic actions.

AAEF does not attempt to define all aspects of AI safety, AI governance, identity, communications, model risk management, or agent interoperability.

## Relationship to AI Governance Frameworks

AI governance frameworks often address lifecycle governance, risk management, accountability, transparency, human oversight, data governance, and organizational policy.

AAEF complements these frameworks by providing a control profile for agentic actions, especially where AI agents can perform actions on behalf of humans or organizations.

## Relationship to Zero Trust Frameworks

Zero Trust frameworks emphasize continuous verification, least privilege, segmentation, monitoring, and explicit trust decisions.

AAEF applies similar thinking to agentic action assurance, but focuses specifically on delegated authority and evidence for actions performed by AI agents.

## Relationship to Identity and Authorization Standards

Identity and authorization standards can provide mechanisms for authentication, token issuance, claims, delegation, and authorization.

AAEF does not replace those mechanisms. Instead, it defines assurance requirements that such mechanisms may help implement.

AAEF asks whether an organization can prove:

- which agent acted,
- on whose behalf,
- under what authority,
- with what authorization decision,
- and with what evidence.

## Relationship to Agent Communication Protocols

Agent communication protocols may define how agents discover, communicate, or exchange tasks.

AAEF focuses on what must be controlled and evidenced when such communication leads to meaningful action.

## Relationship to Threat Taxonomies

Threat taxonomies identify what can go wrong.

AAEF defines controls and evidence expectations for preventing, limiting, detecting, and reviewing those failures when they affect authority and action.

## Suggested Mapping Areas

Future versions of AAEF may include formal mappings to:

- AI governance frameworks,
- Zero Trust control models,
- agentic AI threat taxonomies,
- identity and authorization standards,
- agent communication standards,
- cloud security control matrices,
- audit and assurance frameworks,
- and industry-specific AI risk frameworks.

## Complementary Statement

AAEF is best understood as:

> An action assurance control profile for agentic AI systems.

It is not intended to own the full agentic AI security landscape.

It is intended to make one question operationally answerable:

> How can an organization prove that a specific AI agent action was authorized, bounded, attributable, evidenced, and revocable?
